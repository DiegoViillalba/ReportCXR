6. Domain Shift Audit Protocol

Author

Diego Villalba (Applied Research Candidate)

Published

June 25, 2026

Section Summary (Non-Technical)

This section outlines our system stress-testing infrastructure. Rather than hoping the model works when moved to a new hospital, we write a diagnostic program that simulates three types of shifts: scanner machinery changes (adding noise and changing brightness/contrast), language transitions (evaluating Spanish generations), and epidemiological changes (simulating different rates of diseases). We leave placeholders for the exact degradation curves, as this represents our testing protocol.

Overview of the Audit Protocol

To evaluate the system’s readiness for Latin American clinical environments, we construct the DomainShiftAudit suite. This module simulates the three core axes of distributional shift.

Axis A: Acquisition & Equipment Shift

We apply controlled synthetic image perturbations to simulate differences in scanner hardware, sensor noise, or digitization quality (e.g., older equipment in LATAM public hospitals). We sweep over: * Gaussian Noise: Adding noise with standard deviation \(\sigma \in [0, 50]\) in pixel units. * Contrast Scaling: Factors \(c \in [0.5, 2.0]\). * Gamma Correction: \(\gamma \in [0.5, 2.0]\) (simulating exposure discrepancies). * JPEG Compression: Qualities \(q \in [95, 75, 50, 25, 10]\) (simulating network bandwidth saving compression). * Brightness Scaling: Factors \(b \in [0.5, 1.6]\).

Results & Safety Critical Analysis

The results shown in Figure 1 (a) reveal that all perturbations cause less than \(1.3\%\) BERTScore-F1 degradation across the entire tested sweep range—including extreme conditions (such as JPEG quality = 10 or Gaussian noise \(\sigma = 50\)).

While this superficially suggests that the model is extremely robust, a deeper inspection of the generated texts reveals a major clinical safety failure mode: “hallucination towards normal”.

  • Encoder Corruption: Under severe noise or compression, the frozen SigLIP vision encoder’s perceptual representations become corrupted, and it fails to extract useful visual features.
  • Decoder Defaulting: When the visual signal is lost, the decoder defaults to its strong language prior. It generates highly fluent, grammatically correct reports that describe completely normal findings (e.g., “The lungs are clear. There is no pleural effusion or pneumothorax…”).
  • Metric Masking: Because the baseline test set consists primarily of normal cases, these hallucinated “normal” reports align perfectly with the majority class. As a result, the BERTScore-F1 remains high (~0.69) even when the model is completely blind to any pathology present in the image.

Conclusion: The apparent robustness under image acquisition shifts is an artifact of class imbalance. In practice, visual corruption renders the model clinically blind, causing it to miss critical pathologies without warning.

Axis B: Language Shift

Since MedGemma was pre-trained primarily on English medical text, deploying it in a Spanish-speaking hospital represents a severe vocabulary shift.

To evaluate this without local Spanish X-ray datasets, we package a language-shift simulation within our audit tool (src/domain_shift_audit/language_shift.py): 1. Spanish Generation: Prompting the VLM in Spanish (e.g., using target-language system instructions and indication inputs) to generate Spanish Findings. 2. Pseudo-References: Generating Spanish reference findings by automatically translating the ground-truth English references using a translation model (such as Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-es). 3. Semantic Evaluation: Measuring the relative BLEU, ROUGE, and BERTScore degradations between the Spanish predictions and the pseudo-references.

This module is fully integrated as an automated test suite ready to be executed on local GPU resources.

Axis C: Prevalence Shift

Medical centers in LATAM exhibit different prevalence distributions of diseases compared to US datasets (e.g., higher rates of Tuberculosis or Chagas). We simulate this shift by re-weighting our test set using importance sampling. We compute weighted test metrics and report the Effective Sample Size (ESS) to ensure our estimators remain statistically stable:

\[ESS = \frac{\left( \sum_{j=1}^N w_j \right)^2}{\sum_{j=1}^N w_j^2}\]

If the \(ESS\) falls below a safety threshold (e.g., \(ESS < 30\)), we flag the evaluation as statistically unreliable due to extreme distribution divergence.

We swept the target prevalence of four rare pathologies (Pleural Effusion, Atelectasis, Pneumonia, and Consolidation) from \(0\%\) to \(50\%\) and re-evaluated both our uniform (uniform_v3) and weighted (weighted_v4) models.

Results Analysis

The sweeps shown in Figure 2 (a) yield several key insights: 1. Estimation Stability: The dashed lines represent regions where the \(ESS\) falls below 30. As target prevalence pushes toward \(50\%\) (far exceeding the source prevalences: Effusion \(3.2\%\), Atelectasis \(6.7\%\), Pneumonia \(1\%\), Consolidation \(0.7\%\)), the re-weighting estimator becomes highly unstable due to the lack of positive examples in the test set. 2. Uniform vs. Weighted Performance: * Uniform Model (uniform_v3): Performance drops sharply as target prevalence of pathologies increases. This occurs because the uniform model was trained on the raw, normal-heavy distribution and retains a strong bias toward normal reports. * Weighted Model (weighted_v4): Performance remains significantly more stable as pathology prevalence rises. Because the training loop used importance sampler weights, the model learned robust visual-textual associations for rare pathologies, making it more resilient to epidemiological prevalence shifts.

1.  **Estimation Stability**: The dashed lines represent regions where the $ESS$ falls below 30.     *   **Uniform Model (`uniform_v3`)**: Performance drops sharply as target prevalence of pathologies increases. This occurs because the uniform model was trained on the raw, normal-heavy distribution and retains a strong bias toward normal reports.
