1. Introduction & Strategic Thesis

Author

Diego Villalba (Applied Research Candidate)

Published

June 26, 2026

Section Summary (Non-Technical)

This section introduces the core clinical motivation of the project and documents the literature review that shaped the experimental design. Chest X-ray models are typically trained on massive datasets from large US hospitals, which contain mostly English reports and a very high proportion of normal X-rays. Deploying these models in Latin American clinics introduces three types of failure: different image properties from different X-ray machines, different reporting languages (Spanish instead of English), and different rates of disease. We frame this not as a translation or accuracy task, but as a systematic domain-shift diagnostic challenge. The literature review also defines which evaluation metrics are meaningful in a clinical setting—and why standard NLP metrics like BLEU fall short.

1. Clinical Domain Shift

Developing a multimodal medical vision-language foundation model that generates chest X-ray findings is one of the key challenges in medical AI. However, deploying such a model in a real clinical setting—particularly across distinct geographic regions like Latin America—introduces severe out-of-distribution (OOD) shifts:

  1. Acquisition Shift: Differences in scanner hardware manufacturers, kVp exposure calibrations, and sensor resolutions produce raw image distributions that diverge from the distributions encountered during pre-training.
  2. Prevalence Shift: Regional epidemiological patterns result in different baseline frequencies of pathologies. Latin American public clinics may exhibit higher prevalences of Tuberculosis or Chagas disease compared to standard Western benchmarks.
  3. Language Shift: Pre-trained clinical VLMs like MedGemma are pre-trained predominantly on English corpora (e.g., MIMIC-CXR). Clinicians in LATAM dictate findings and indications in Spanish.

2. The Strategic Thesis

Our thesis is that cross-geographic deployment must be framed as a distributional discrepancy audit. Rather than optimising a model to achieve maximum accuracy on a single dataset, we construct a reusable diagnostic infrastructure—the DomainShiftAudit class—capable of auditing models under simulated shifts.

By validating this diagnostic protocol on controlled, synthetic perturbations within the Indiana University Chest X-ray (IU CXR) dataset, we ensure that the auditing infrastructure is fully developed and ready to be run on real local clinical data from day one.

3. Literature Survey: Clinical Accuracy in VLMs for Radiology

Before defining our experimental design, I conducted a structured survey of recent advances in vision-language models for chest X-ray report generation, anchored in the MedGemma technical report (Sellergren et al. 2025) as the foundation for understanding the model family we fine-tune.

The central observation from this survey is that the field has pivoted away from standard NLP metrics (BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE) toward metrics that measure clinical accuracy and logical consistency. The papers below represent the frontier of this pivot.

3.1 Survey Table

Paper Key Contribution
MedGemma (Sellergren et al. 2025) Medical VLM pre-trained on clinical images and text; frozen SigLIP encoder + Gemma decoder. Establishes the base model for this project.
EditGRPO (K. Zhang et al. 2025) Post-Rollout Edit strategy with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to correct clinical errors in generated drafts without full retraining.
CCS (X. Zhang et al. 2026) Generates multiple report candidates and applies a clinical consensus mechanism to select the one with highest cross-candidate agreement.
Formal Verification (Singh et al. 2026) Applies formal logic to verify that a model’s diagnostic impression is logically entailed by its own perceptual findings, preventing self-contradiction hallucinations.
SRRG (Kang et al. 2025) Generates reports in a structured format (SRRG) with rich patient context, improving diagnostic relevance and radiologist verifiability.
RadAlign (Gu et al. 2025) Vision-language concept alignment to bridge the gap between image region features and medical terminology—targeting the vocabulary mismatch problem.
EviAgent (Qi et al. 2026) Agentic framework that requires the model to cite specific visual evidence before making any diagnostic claim, enforcing grounded reasoning.
RadVLM (Vandenhirtz et al. 2025) Multitask conversational model trained on chest X-rays for both report generation and free-form clinical queries.

3.2 Notable Trends

Three structural patterns emerge across this literature:

Clinical consistency over lexical overlap. Models are increasingly evaluated with CheXbert (Chen et al. 2024) and RadGraph, which extract clinical entities (e.g., “pleural effusion,” “cardiomegaly”) from both generated and reference reports and measure F1 over those entities—rather than counting matched word n-grams. This is the direct motivation for choosing CheXbert macro-F1 as our primary metric.

Rare-label coverage as a first-class objective. Standard accuracy metrics mask performance collapse on infrequent pathologies because the test set is dominated by normal studies. Several of these papers introduce macro-averaged or weighted metrics that give equal weight to each pathology class regardless of prevalence. This drove our use of macro-F1 over micro-F1 as the primary signal for model comparison.

Structured versus free-text generation. There is a growing shift toward Structured Radiology Report Generation (SRRG) as seen in (Kang et al. 2025), which makes reports more interoperable and easier to audit. While we generate free-text Findings sections to match the IU CXR dataset format, this trend validates using CheXbert label extraction as a post-hoc structuring layer for evaluation.

4. Metric Selection Rationale

The literature survey directly informed our choice of evaluation metrics. This section makes those choices explicit.

4.1 Why Not Standard NLP Metrics as Primary?

BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L measure n-gram overlap between the generated and reference text. In radiology report generation, this is insufficient:

  • A report saying “The lungs are clear. No pleural effusion.” and a report saying “Clear lungs. Pleural effusion is absent.” are clinically identical but score poorly on n-gram overlap.
  • A report that hallucinates a plausible-sounding finding can still score high on BLEU if the writing style matches the reference.
  • These metrics do not penalise a model for missing pathology labels the reference mentions.

We retain BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L as secondary fluency metrics to characterise the style-vs-accuracy trade-off seen in RAG.

4.2 Primary Metric: CheXbert Macro-F1

CheXbert (Chen et al. 2024) is a fine-tuned classifier that extracts a 14-dimensional binary label vector from free-text radiology reports. By running it on both the generated report and the ground-truth report and computing F1 at the label level, we get a pathology-aware measure.

We use the macro-averaged variant (equal weight per class regardless of prevalence) as our primary metric because:

  • The IU CXR dataset is 83% “No Finding”. Micro-F1 would be dominated by the normal class.
  • A model that predicts “No Finding” for every study achieves high micro-F1 but zero macro-F1—exactly the zero-shot MedGemma behaviour we observe.
  • The challenge is rare-label detection: Edema (2.3%), Consolidation (0.7%), Pneumonia (1.0%). Macro-F1 forces every label to contribute equally to the final score.

4.3 Secondary Metric: BERTScore-F1

BERTScore (Sellergren et al. 2025) measures token-level semantic similarity using contextual embeddings rather than exact word matches. We use microsoft/deberta-xlarge-mnli as the scoring model, which was chosen for its strong natural language inference capabilities relevant to clinical text.

BERTScore is used as the fluency and semantic quality signal. It captures whether the generated report conveys the right clinical concepts even when phrased differently from the reference, and it is robust to paraphrasing in a way BLEU is not. Its limitation is that it does not directly measure pathology-level recall—two reports can have similar BERTScore while differing entirely on which labels are present.

4.4 Summary

Metric Type What it measures Role
CheXbert macro-F1 Clinical label recall Pathology detection (equal weight per class) Primary
CheXbert micro-F1 Clinical label accuracy Overall label accuracy (prevalence-weighted) Supporting
BERTScore-F1 Semantic similarity Report fluency and concept coverage Secondary
BLEU-4 Lexical overlap Writing style proximity to reference Secondary
ROUGE-L Sequence overlap Longest common subsequence match Secondary

This metric stack allows us to characterise each experimental configuration along two independent axes—clinical precision (CheXbert) and textual quality (BERTScore/BLEU)—which is exactly the Pareto trade-off observed across the ten inference configurations in Section 5.
