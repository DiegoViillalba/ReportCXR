1. Introduction & Strategic Thesis

Author

Diego Villalba (Applied Research Candidate)

Published

June 25, 2026

Section Summary (Non-Technical)

This section introduces the core clinical motivation of the project. Chest X-ray models are usually trained on massive datasets from large US hospitals, which contain mostly English reports and a very high percentage of normal X-rays. When trying to deploy these models in Latin American (LATAM) clinics, they face three major changes: different image properties from different X-ray machines, different languages (Spanish instead of English), and different rates of disease. We frame this problem not as a simple translation or accuracy task, but as a systematic domain shift diagnostic challenge.

Clinical Domain Shift

The Eden AI technical challenge focuses on developing a multimodal medical vision-language foundation model that generates chest X-ray findings. However, deploying such a model in a real clinical setting—particularly across distinct geographic regions like Latin America—introduces severe out-of-distribution (OOD) shifts:

  1. Acquisition Shift: Differences in scanner hardware manufacturers, kVp exposure calibrations, and sensor resolutions produce raw image distributions that diverge from the clean datasets used during pre-training.
  2. Prevalence Shift: Regional epidemiological patterns result in different baseline frequencies of pathologies. For example, Latin American public clinics may exhibit higher prevalences of Tuberculosis or Chagas disease compared to standard Western benchmarks.
  3. Language Shift: Pre-trained clinical vision-language models like MedGemma are pre-trained predominantly on English corpora (e.g., MIMIC-CXR). Clinicians in LATAM dictate findings and indications in Spanish.

The Strategic Thesis

Our thesis is that cross-geographic deployment must be framed as a distributional discrepancy audit. Rather than optimizing a model to achieve maximum accuracy on a single dataset, we construct a reusable diagnostic infrastructure—the DomainShiftAudit class—capable of auditing models under simulated shifts.

By validating this diagnostic protocol on controlled, synthetic perturbations within the Indiana University Chest X-ray (IU CXR) dataset, we ensure that the auditing infrastructure is fully developed and ready to be run on real, local clinical data from day one.

## Clinical Domain Shift

The Eden AI technical challenge focuses on developing a multimodal medical vision-language foundation model that generates chest X-ray findings. However, deploying such a model in a real clinical setting—particularly across distinct geographic regions like Latin America—introduces severe out-of-distribution (OOD) shifts:

1.  **Acquisition Shift**: Differences in scanner hardware manufacturers, kVp exposure calibrations, and sensor resolutions produce raw image distributions that diverge from the clean datasets used during pre-training.
2.  **Prevalence Shift**: Regional epidemiological patterns result in different baseline frequencies of pathologies. For example, Latin American public clinics may exhibit higher prevalences of Tuberculosis or Chagas disease compared to standard Western benchmarks.
3.  **Language Shift**: Pre-trained clinical vision-language models like MedGemma are pre-trained predominantly on English corpora (e.g., MIMIC-CXR). Clinicians in LATAM dictate findings and indications in Spanish.

## The Strategic Thesis

Our thesis is that **cross-geographic deployment must be framed as a distributional discrepancy audit**. Rather than optimizing a model to achieve maximum accuracy on a single dataset, we construct a reusable diagnostic infrastructure—the `DomainShiftAudit` class—capable of auditing models under simulated shifts. 

By validating this diagnostic protocol on controlled, synthetic perturbations within the Indiana University Chest X-ray (IU CXR) dataset, we ensure that the auditing infrastructure is fully developed and ready to be run on real, local clinical data from day one.
